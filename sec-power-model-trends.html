    Section 17.2 Power Model Trends Activities

    We’ve seen examples of power models with a variety of exponents. Once we’ve decided a power model is appropriate, we can determine whether the exponent should be positive or negative by looking at how the quantities change together.
    🔗
    • If they increase together, then we need a positive power.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If as one quantity increases, the other decreases, then we need a negative power.
      🔗
      🔗
    But, if all we have is data, how do we know if a power model is appropriate? And, how do we figure out what the value of the exponent should be? In this lesson, we’ll learn how to use a log-log graph to answer both of these questions.
    🔗

    Activity 17.2.1.

    In order to understand why logs are helpful here and also see how to use them effectively, we need to start by doing some algebra.
    🔗

    (a)

    Let’s look at an example, using the specific power model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} d = 0.05v^2 \end{equation*}
    that we found in Activity 12.2.1 for the relationship between the braking distance and speed of a car.
    🔗
    1. Take \(\log_{10}\) of both sides to get
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \log_{10}(d) = \log_{10}(0.05 \times v^2) \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. Use the log property
      \begin{equation*} \log(xy) = \log(x) + \log(y) \end{equation*}
      that we introduced in Activity 13.2.5 to rewrite the right hand side:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \log_{10}(d) = \log_{10}(0.05) + \log_{10}(v^2) \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Use a calculator to find \(\log_{10}(0.05)\text{,}\) and use the log property
      \begin{equation*} \log(x^r) = r\log(x) \end{equation*}
      to rewrite the hand side again:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \log_{10}(d) = -1.301 + 2\log_{10}(v) \end{equation*}
      🔗
    We’ve rewritten our original power model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} d = 0.05v^2 \end{equation*}
    as a linear model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \log_{10}(d) = -1.301 + 2\log_{10}(v) \end{equation*}
    for \(\log_{10}\) of both of our original variables, and the slope of the linear model is our exponent.
    🔗
    Now you try: mimic the steps from the example to rewrite the power model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} R = 70 \times M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    from Activity 14.2.4 as a linear model for \(\log_{10}\) of \(R\) and \(M\text{.}\) Is the slope of the linear model equal to \(\frac34\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Now consider the generic power model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Q_2 = c Q_1^p \end{equation*}
    where \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) are the quantities we’re interested in and are the variables when we work with a specific power model, and \(c\) and \(p\) are numbers. Use the same steps from the previous part of this activity to rewrite the model as a linear model for \(\log_{10}\) of the variables.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    What do you notice about the slope of the linear model for \(\log_{10}\) of the variables?
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Activity 17.2.3.

    In Activity 16.2.1, we worked with Zipf’s Law in linguistics, which says that in a text
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{# of times a word appears} \approx \frac{c}{\text{rank}} \end{equation*}
    where the rank of the most common word is 1, the second most common word is 2, etc. and \(c\) is some constant. It has been suggested
     4 
    cf. Sidra Arshad, Shougeng Hu, and Badar Nadeem Ashraf. (2018) “Zipf’s law and city size distribution: A survey of the literature and future research agenda”. Physica A: Statistical Mechanics and its Applications. v. 492, pp. 75 - 92. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physa.2017.10.005.
    that Zipf’s Law also applies to the distribution of city sizes. That is, if \(P\) is a city’s population and \(r\) is its size rank then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P \approx \frac{c}{r}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let’s explore this claim using US cities. The table below gives 2025 estimates for the population (within the official city limits) of the 10 largest cities in the US
     5 
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_United_States_cities_by_population
    .

    Activity 17.2.4.

    In the late 19th century, Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto claimed that the income distribution in all countries followed a power model
     6 
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vilfredo_Pareto#Pareto_theory_of_maximum_economics
    . That is, if \(x\) is an amount of money and \(N\) is the number of people who earn at least \(x\) in a year, then
    \begin{equation*} N = cx^p \end{equation*}
    where \(c\) and \(p\) are numbers that might be different in different countries and at different times. Modern economists have modified Pareto’s claim: instead of the entire income distribution following a power model, they suggest that lower incomes (i.e., the incomes of most people) follow a different pattern, but very high incomes follow a power model. Let’s investigate these claims for the United States in the 21st century, using IRS data in the table below
     7 
    “All Returns: Sources of Income, Adjustments Deductions and Exemptions, and Tax Items. Published as: Individual Complete Report (Publication 1304), Table 1.4” (2023), SOI Tax Stats, IRS.
    .
    🔗

    (a)

    Use a calculator to fill in the Log(AGI) and Log(Num) columns in the table.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Make a log-log graph for the data.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    If modern economists are correct, then your log-log graph should suggest that a linear model is a good fit for the log-log data for high income values, but that a different pattern holds for the lower income values. Does this seem to be the case? Explain your answer.

    Find a linear model for the log-log data for high income values.
