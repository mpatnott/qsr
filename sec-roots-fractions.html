    Section 14.2 Roots and Fractional Powers Activities

    Activity 14.2.1.

    We already know that \(x^2 = x\cdot x\text{,}\) \(x^3 = x\cdot x \cdot x\text{,}\) etc. We also know that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x}\sqrt{x} = x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt[3]{x}\sqrt[3]{x}\sqrt[3]{x} = x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt[4]{x}\sqrt[4]{x}\sqrt[4]{x}\sqrt[4]{x} = x \end{equation*}
    and so on.
    🔗
    Thinking about exponents as repeated multiplication only works when the power is a whole number! How can we think about what it means to take the \(\frac13\)rd power, like in Prep Activity 14.1.1? The exponent properties can help us!
    🔗

    (a)

    Use the exponent properties to rewrite each of the following expressions as \(x^{?}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/2}x^{1/2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/3}x^{1/3}x^{1/3} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^{1/4}x^{1/4}x^{1/4}x^{1/4} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    (b)

    What does your work suggest about the relationship between \(x^{1/2}\) and \(\sqrt{x}\text{?}\) \(x^{1/3}\) and \(\sqrt[3]{x}\text{?}\) \(x^{1/4}\) and \(\sqrt[4]{x}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    (i)
    Rewrite with fractional exponents: \(\sqrt{x^3} + \sqrt[3]{x^2}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (ii)
    Rewrite with fractional exponents: \(\sqrt[3]{2 + x^5}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (iii)
    Rewrite with fractional exponents: \(\frac{\sqrt[4]{x^7}}{\sqrt[4]{x}}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (iv)
    Rewrite with roots: \(\left(512x^6\right)^{2/9}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    (v)
    Rewrite with roots: \(\left(x-5\right)^{4/7}\)
    🔗

    Activity 14.2.2.

    In Activity 12.2.1, we discovered that the model
    \begin{equation*} D = 0.05v^2 \end{equation*}
    let’s us estimate the braking distance (in feet), \(D\text{,}\) required to stop a car going \(v\) mph on dry asphalt.
    🔗
    Jeb is analyzing data on car accidents that includes the length of skid marks made by cars when they braked suddenly. He wants to use the skid mark data to estimate the car’s speed when they hit the brakes. He’s going to assume that skid marks are the same as the braking distance of the car.
    🔗

    (a)

    Why can Jeb use our braking distance model to help him solve his problem?
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    One car’s skid marks were \(135\) ft long. Use the model to estimate its speed.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Jeb doesn’t want to have to do algebra and solve for \(v\) for every car in his data set. Instead, he’d prefer to have a model that outputs \(v\text{,}\) the speed in mph, when given \(D\text{,}\) the length of the skid marks in feet. Mimic the algebra steps you did to find the speed when \(D = 135\) to rewrite our model so that it looks like \(v = \text{formula}\) (that is, to solve the model for \(v\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Check that your rewritten model works by plugging in \(135\) for \(D\text{.}\) You should get the same value for \(v\text{!}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    Use your rewritten model to find the speed of a car that made skid marks 215 feet long.
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    Your rewritten model is a power model! Use the rule you discovered in Activity 14.2.1 to rewrite the root as a fractional power, if you haven’t yet.

    Activity 14.2.3.

    In Activity 12.2.4, we learned that for many countries,
    \begin{equation*} P \approx s^3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(P\) is the population of a country and \(s\) is the size of its equivalent to the US House of Representatives.
    🔗
    It might seem a little strange to use the size of the House of Representatives to predict the size of the population! It would feel more natural to most people to instead use the size of the population to predict the size of the House. In fact, political scientists usually state this guideline that way.
    🔗
    Use what you’ve learned about roots and fractional exponents to rewrite \(P = s^3\) as a power model in the form \(s = P^?\) and to explain why political scientists sometimes call this model the “cube root law”.
    🔗
    🔗

    Activity 14.2.4.

    The resting energy expenditure (REE) is the amount of energy a person or animal needs for basic bodily functions. In the 1930s, biologist Max Kleiber
     1 
    Z. Wang, T. O’Connor, S. Heshka, and s. Heymsfield. ``The Reconstruction of Kleiber’s Law at the Organ-Tissue Level.’’ The Journal of Nutrition v. 131, no. 11. doi: 10.1093/jn/131.11.2967
    discovered that there is a relationship between the REE of an adult mammal and its body mass:
    \begin{equation*} R = 70 \times M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    where \(R\) is a mammal’s REE (in kcal/day) and \(M\) is its body mass (in kg).
    🔗

    (a)

    Explain why Kleiber’s model for REE is a power model. What are the quantities, coefficient, and power?
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    A typical cat has a body mass of 3.5 kg. Use this model to estimate the REE for a cat.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    In order to find the body mass of a mammal with REE 1400 kcal/d, we need to undo the 3/4ths power. We don’t usually talk about the 3/4ths root. Instead, we stick with fractional exponents. To undo the 3/4ths power, we’ll use the
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3/4}\text{ths power}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Here’s how this example works out!
    \begin{equation*} 1400 = 70 \times M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    Divide both sides by 70 to get:
    \begin{equation*} 20 = M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    Raise both sides to the 1/(3/4)ths power:
    \begin{equation*} 20^{1/(3/4)} = \left(M^{3/4}\right)^{1/(3/4)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 54.29 = M \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note: 1/(3/4) is hard to read and work with! It’s important to get the parentheses right, since 1/(3/4) and (1/3)/4 are different (try to plug both into a calculator and see for yourself!). Instead of doing 1/(3/4), it’s more common to either turn 3/4 into a decimal and do 1/0.75, or to use fraction arithmetic rules to rewrite 1/(3/4) as 4/3.
    🔗
    A rabbit has a REE of about 145 kcal/day. Use this model to estimate the rabbit’s mass.
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    An elephant has a REE of about 39,000 kcal/day. Use this model to estimate the elephant’s mass.
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    How many times bigger than a cat is an elephant? Divide the elephant’s mass by the cat’s mass to find out.
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    How many more food does an elephant need in a day than a cat? Divide the elephant’s REE by the cat’s REE to find out.
    🔗
    🔗

    (g)

    What do you notice about your last two answers?

    🔗
