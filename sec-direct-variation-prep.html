    Section 12.1 Proportionality and Direct Variation Prep Activities

    Two varying quantities \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) have a proportional relationship when the ratio between the quantities is constant. That is, \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) have a proportional relationship when
    \begin{equation*} c = \frac{Q_2}{Q_1} \end{equation*}
    always has the same value. We call \(c\) the constant of proportionality.
    🔗

    Prep Activity 12.1.1.

    Peyton is using an app to track data about their recent activity. The tables below show time, \(T\text{,}\) (measured in minutes) and distance, \(D\) from their starting location (measured in miles) data from three of Peyton’s recent activity sessions.
    🔗

    (a)

    Find \(\frac{D}{T}\) for each row in the Walk table. Use your results to determine whether Peyton’s time and distance on their walk have a proportional relationship.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find \(\frac{D}{T}\) for each row in the Hike table. Use your results to determine whether Peyton’s time and distance on their hike have a proportional relationship.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Find \(\frac{D}{T}\) for each row in the Bike Ride table. Use your results to determine whether Peyton’s time and distance on their bike ride have a proportional relationship.
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    What is the constant of proportionality for each of the activities in which Peyton’s time and distance had a proportional relationship?
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    Interpret the constant of proportionality in context. That is, what does it tell us about the relationship between Peyton’s time and distance?

    Prep Activity 12.1.2.

    When two quantities have a proportional relationship, it is often useful to rewrite the equation
    \begin{equation*} c = \frac{Q_2}{Q_1} \end{equation*}
    as
    \begin{equation*} Q_2 = c Q_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We sometimes say that \(Q_2\) varies directly with respect to \(Q_1\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (a)

    Use your work from Prep Activity 12.1.1 to write an equation that shows that Peyton’s distance varies directly with respect to time for one of their activities.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Use your equation to predict Peyton’s distance after 65 minutes of activity.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Use your equation to predict how long it would take Peyton to go 3.0 miles.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
