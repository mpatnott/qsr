    Section 12.2 Proportionality and Direct Variation

    Recall that in Section 12.1, we said that two varying quantities \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) have a proportional relationship when the ratio between the quantities is constant. That is, \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) have a proportional relationship when
    \begin{equation*} c = \frac{Q_2}{Q_1} \end{equation*}
    always has the same value. We called \(c\) the constant of proportionality. We also noted that it is sometimes useful to rewrite this equation as
    \begin{equation*} Q_2 = c Q_1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and say that \(Q_2\) varies directly with respect to \(Q_1\text{.}\)

    Activity 12.2.2.

    A power model is a relationship between two quantities that can be written in the form
    \begin{equation*} Q_2 = c \times Q_1^p\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) are the two quantities, \(c\) is a number called the coefficient and \(p\) is a number called the power. The quantitites \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) are variables, and the coefficient and the power are numbers.
    🔗
    The formula you found in Activity 12.2.1 was a power model, with
    \begin{equation*} Q_1 = v, Q_2 = d, p = 2, c = 0.05\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The power \(p\) can be ANY number, including negatives, fractions, and decimals.

    Activity 12.2.3.

    We saw in Activity 12.2.1 that the braking distance, \(d\text{,}\) of a car varies directly with the square of its speed, \(v\text{.}\) That is, \(d = cv^2\) for some constant of proportionality \(c\text{.}\) We used data to find that when \(v\) is measured in mph, \(d\) is measured in feet, and the car is driving on dry asphalt, \(c = 0.05\text{ feet}/\text{mph}^2\text{.}\) This let us set up the equation \(d = 0.05v^2\) and use it to make predictions. Notice that this equation is a power model!
