    Section 5.3 Quadratic and Polynomial Models

    Activity 5.3.1.

    Edna makes ceramic gnomes to sell at a local craft market each week. The cost of her ceramic-making equipment is $100 and the cost of supplies per gnome is $15. If the selling price per gnome is $50 then Edna will sell 28 gnomes per week but if she drops the price to $45 per gnome then she will sell 32 of them per week.

    (a)

    A price-demand function is a mathematical model the describes the relationship between the selling price of an item and the demand, i.e., the number of items sold. Let \(p\) be the selling price for one of Edna’s gnomes and \(x\) be the number of gnomes Edna sells. Assume that the relationship between \(p\) and \(x\) is linear, and use the information given in the problem statement to find a linear model for the price-demand function for Edna’s gnomes.

    (b)

    You should have found a negative slope for your price-demand function. Why does it make sense that the slope would be negative in this context? That is, why does it make sense that the price-demand function is decreasing?

    (c)

    Let \(R\) be Edna’s revenue, the amount of money that she takes in from selling gnomes. Explain why it makes sense that \(R = xp\text{.}\)

    (d)

    The model \(R = xp\) is a multivariable model for revenue. It’s often useful to turn multivariable models into single variable models, when possible, because many of the mathematical tools we use to analyze models are simpler when there are fewer variables. We can get a model for revenue that includes only \(R\) and \(x\) (the number of items sold) by replacing \(p\) with the price-demand function.
    For example, Manuel sells handmade cutting boards at the craft fair. His price-demand function for his cutting boards is
    \begin{equation*} p = 90 - 2(x - 17)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    That means his revenue is
    \begin{equation*} R = x(90 - 2(x-17))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note: The parentheses are important! In order to find revenue, we need to multiply the number of items sold by the price of an item. If Manuel wrote his revenue model as
    \begin{equation*} R = x\times 90 - 2(x-17)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then his model would find number of items sold times 90, then subtract \(2(x-17)\text{,}\) instead of number of items sold times that price of the item (which is \(90 - 2(x-17)\)).
    Find a single variable model for Edna’s revenue by replacing \(p\) with your price-demand function.

    (e)

    Manuel’s revenue model is kind of messy! He decides to clean it up using distribution. First, he uses the distributive property to rewrite \(-2(x - 17) = -2x + 34\text{.}\) This gives him
    \begin{equation*} R = x(90 - 2x + 34)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which he simplifies by adding 90 and 34 to get
    \begin{equation*} R = x(124 - 2x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Finally, Manuel distributes the \(x\) to get
    \begin{equation*} R = 124x - 2x^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Use the distributive property to clean up your model for Edna’s revenue.

    (f)

    What will Edna’s revenue be is she sells 20 gnomes? 30 gnomes? 40 gnomes?

    (g)

    Use Desmos to create a graph for Edna’s revenue. Sketch your graph here. What do you notice about the graph? What do you wonder?

    (h)

    Explain how you know that this model for revenue is not a linear, exponential, or power model.

    (i)

    Use Desmos and your graph to find the number of gnomes that Edna needs to sell in order to have revenue of exactly $1200. You should have two answers!

    (j)

    Try to find the number of gnomes that Edna needs to sell in order to have revenue of exactly $1200 using algebra instead of the graph. What problem(s) do you encounter? Or, what tool that we haven’t discussed yet did you need to use?

    (k)

    Use Desmos and your graph to find the number of gnomes that Edna needs to sell in order to have revenue of at least $1200. Your answer should be a range of numbers!

    Activity 5.3.2.

    The models we found for revenue in Activity 5.3.1 are polynomials. A polynomial model can be written as a sum of nonnegative whole number powers of a variable times constants. In formal symbols, we can write a polynomial in the form
    \begin{equation*} a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_2 x^2 + a_1 x + a_0 \end{equation*}
    for some nonegative whole number \(n\) and numbers \(a_n, a_{n-1}, \ldots, a_1, a_0\text{.}\) The numbers that are multiplied by the variable and its powers are called coefficients.
    The highest power in a polynomial is called the degree of the polynomial. The leading coefficient is the coefficient of the highest power. A polynomial of degree 2 is also called a quadratic model.

    (a)

    Decide whether or not each of the expressions below is a polynomial. Briefly explain your answers.
    (i)
    \(7x^3 - 8\)
    (ii)
    \(5x^8\)
    (iii)
    \(6x^{-6} - 9x^2 - 8\)
    (iv)
    \(9^x - 7x^9\)
    (v)
    \(7x^{1/3} + 1\)
    (vi)
    \(9 - \frac43 x^2 + 4x\)

    (b)

    What are the degrees and leading coefficients of the expressions from Task 5.3.2.a that were polynomial?

    (c)

    Are any of the expressions in Task 5.3.2.a quadratic models?

    (d)

    Is a linear model a polynomial? Explain your answer.

    (e)

    Are power models polynomials? Explain your answer.

    (f)

    Write a list of features that tell you that an expression is NOT a polynomial.

    Activity 5.3.4.

    In Activity 5.3.1, we looked at Edna’s small business selling ceramic gnomes at craft fairs. In that activity, we explored her revenue. Edna is more interested in her profit than her revenue. Profit is the amount of money that she’s made after subtracting her costs from her revenue.

    (a)

    Use the information given in Activity 5.3.1 to find a model for Edna’s costs, \(C\text{,}\) to produce \(x\) gnomes. Additionally, explain how we know that a linear model is appropriate.

    (b)

    You should have found a positive slope for your cost model. Why does it make sense that the slope would be positive in this context? That is, why does it make sense that the cost model is increasing?

    (c)

    Let \(P\) be Edna’s profit. Profit is revenue minus costs. Use your revenue model from Activity 5.3.1 and your cost model to find a model for profit.

    (d)

    Is your profit model any of: linear, exponential, power, quadratic, or polynomial? Explain how you know.

    (e)

    Use Desmos to graph your profit model, and sketch your graph here. What do you notice? What do you wonder?

    (f)

    Use Desmos and your graph to determine how many gnomes Edna needs to sell in order to at least breakeven, i.e., in order to have a profit of $0 or higher. Your answer should be a range of numbers!

    (g)

    Use Desmos and your graph to determine how many gnomes Edna needs to sell in order to maximize her profit.

    (h)

    Use your price-demand function from Activity 5.3.1 to determine the range of prices that Edna should consider for her gnomes in order to at least breakeven, and the price that will give her the highest profit.

    Activity 5.3.5.

    In Activity 5.3.3, you helped Pablo and Aisha build a box out of cardstock with the largest volume possible. At the event, Pablo noticed that most people didn’t just fill their box to the top, but piled up candy up higher than the top of the box. He and Aisha want to reconsider their box design to take into account this additional candy. They want to maximize the volume, including not only the box but also the pile on top.

    (a)

    Is the length or width of the box going to be smaller? How do you know?

    (b)

    Pablo thinks that most people’s extra candy pile was roughly pyramid-shaped. The base of the period is the rectangular opening at the top of the box. Find a formula for the area of the base.

    (c)

    Pablo thinks the height of most people’s pyramid of candy was about a quarter of the shorterst side of the base. Find a formula for the height of the pyramid.

    (d)

    The volume of a pyramid is
    \begin{equation*} \frac13 \times (\text{Area of Base}) \times \text{Height}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Find a formula for the volume of the pyramid.

    (e)

    Find a formula for the total volume of candy by adding your formula for the volume of the box from Activity 5.3.3 and your formula for the volume of the pyramid.

    (f)

    What should \(x\) be in order to make the total volume as large as possible? Hint: Use Desmos to help you answer this question.
