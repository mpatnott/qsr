    Prep Activity 17.1.1.

    The table below shows the average price per pound ($/lb) of 100% beef ground chuck in the US in May
     1 
    https://www.bls.gov/charts/consumer-price-index/consumer-price-index-average-price-data.htm
    🔗

    (a)

    Sketch a graph showing this data. (Start by setting up axes! Do you need to include any negatives?)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find a formula for a linear model for this data. (If you’re stuck, start by finding an average rate of change to use as your slope estimate. Then, ask yourself: what else do I need to finish this model?)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Use your model to estimate the average cost of ground chuck in 2010.
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    The actual average cost in 2010 was $2.921/lb. How well did your model do?
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    Use your model to estimate when the average cost of ground chuck was $5/lb.
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    The actual average cost in 2022 was $4.894, in 2023 was $4.953, and in 2024 was $5.331. How well did you model do?
    🔗

    Prep Activity 17.1.2.

    Although a linear model is a reasonable fit for the ground chuck cost data, an exponential model is better. Let’s review finding and using exponential models.
    🔗

    (a)

    First, we find the \(\log_{10}\) of the cost only. Use your calculator to fill in these values in the table below.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Sketch a new graph that shows the years on the horizontal axis, and Log($/lb) on the vertical axis. This type of graph is sometimes called a semi-log graph, since one of the axes is the log of the data.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Find a linear model for Log($/lb) with respect to year. (If you’re stuck, ask yourself: what information do I need in order to build a linear model?)
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Use your linear model for Log($/lb) to estimate \(\log_{10}(\text{cost in 2010})\text{.}\) Then, raise 10 to the power of your answer in order to get an estimate for the cost in 2010. How did this model do?
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    In order to use this model to estimate when the cost of ground chuck was $5/lb, start by finding \(\log_{10}(5).\text{.}\) Then, plug \(\log_{10}(5)\) into the appropriate place in your model formula and use algebra to estimate the year. How did the model do on this estimate?
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    In order to use this model to estimate the cost in a given year, we always have to raise 10 to the power of the output of our model. Sometimes, it’s convenient to rewrite the model in exponential form, as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C = 10^{mt + b} \end{equation*}
    where \(C\) is the cost in $/lb and \(mt + b\) your linear model for Log($/lb). Write your model in this format.
    🔗
    🔗
