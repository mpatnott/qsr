    Section 5.2 Power Models

    Activity 5.2.1.

    The volume of a cube can be modeled by the formula
    \begin{equation*} V = s^3 \end{equation*}
    where \(s\) is the length of a side of the cube and \(V\) is the cube’s volume.

    (a)

    Explain why the volume of a cube is a power model, and identify its quantities, coefficient, and power.

    (b)

    Sketch a graph showing the relationship between the volume of a cube and its side lengths.

    (c)

    Confirm that the volume of a cube is not a linear model using average rates of change.

    (d)

    Confirm that the volume of a cube is not an exponential model using relative change.

    (e)

    In order to find the side length of a cube with volume \(8 \text{m}^3\text{,}\) we can use one of two equivalent strategies:
    1. Find the cube root of 8, \(\sqrt[3]{8}\text{.}\)
    2. Find the \(\frac{1}{3}\) power of 8, \(8^{1/3}\text{.}\)
    Find the side length of a cube with volume \(10 \text{ ft}^3\)

    Activity 5.2.2.

    The cube root law
     1 
    R. Taagepera. ``The size of national assemblies.’’ Social Science Research v. 1, no. 4. https://doi.org/10.1016/0049-089X(72)90084-1
    , in political science, observes that in many countries, the number of seats in the legislative body most similar to the US House of Representatives is approximately modeled by the formula \(s = \sqrt[3]{P}\text{,}\) where \(s\) is the number of seats in the legislative body and \(P\) is the country’s population.

    (a)

    The cube root law is a power model, even though it doesn’t look like one at first. Use the fact that cube roots are the same as the 1/3rd power to rewrite the cube root law so that it looks like a power model.

    (b)

    The population of Canada was about 37 million in 2021. The Canadian House of Commons is similar to the US House of Representatives. Use the cube root law to predict the size of the Canadian House of Commons.

    (c)

    In 2021, the Canadian House of Commons had 338 seats. Did the cube root law give a good estimate?

    (d)

    The population of the United States was about 332 million in 2021. Use the cube root law to predict the size of the US House of Representatives.

    (e)

    The US House of Representatives has 435 voting members. Did the cube root law give a good estimate?

    (f)

    Use the cube root law to predict the population of a country with 435 members in its legistlative House.

    (g)

    The US House of Representatives hasn’t changed size since it was set to 435 by the Apportionment Act of 1911. The US population in 1911 was about 94 million. Did the cube root law give a give estimate in 1911?

    Activity 5.2.3.

    The resting energy expenditure (REE) is the amount of energy a person or animal needs for basic bodily functions. In the 1930s, biologist Max Kleiber
     2 
    Z. Wang, T. O’Connor, S. Heshka, and s. Heymsfield. ``The Reconstruction of Kleiber’s Law at the Organ-Tissue Level.’’ The Journal of Nutrition v. 131, no. 11. doi: 10.1093/jn/131.11.2967
    discovered that there is a relationship between the REE of an adult mammal and its body mass:
    \begin{equation*} R = 70 \times M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    where \(R\) is a mammal’s REE (in kcal/d) and \(M\) is its body mass (in kg).

    (a)

    Explain why Kleiber’s model for REE is a power model. What are the quantities, coefficient, and power?

    (b)

    Sketch a graph showing the relationship between REE and body mass in mammals.

    (c)

    Use this model to find the REE of a mammal with body mass 80 kg.

    (d)

    In order to find the body mass of a mammal with REE 1400 kcal/d, we need to undo the 3/4ths power. We don’t usually talk about the 3/4ths root. Instead, we stick with fractional exponents. To undo the 3/4ths power, we’ll use the 1/(3/4)ths power. Here’s how this example works out!
    \begin{equation*} 1400 = 70 \times M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    Divide both sides by 70 to get:
    \begin{equation*} 20 = M^{3/4} \end{equation*}
    Raise both sides to the 1/(3/4)ths power:
    \begin{equation*} 20^{1/(3/4)} = \left(M^{3/4}\right)^{1/(3/4)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 54.29 = M \end{equation*}
    Note: 1/(3/4) is hard to read and work with! It’s important to get the parentheses right, since 1/(3/4) and (1/3)/4 are different (try to plug both into a calculator and see for yourself!). Instead of doing 1/(3/4), it’s more common to either turn 3/4 into a decimal and do 1/0.75, or to use fraction arithmetic rules to rewrite 1/(3/4) as 4/3.
    Find the mass of a mammal with REE 2000 kcal/d.

    Activity 5.2.4.

    Kepler’s Third Law tells us that
    \begin{equation*} 133404 \times a^3 = T^2 \end{equation*}
    where \(a\) is the planet’s distance from the Sun in AU and \(T\) is the number of days the planet takes to orbit the Sun. Note that 1 AU is the distance between Earth and the Sun.

    (a)

    Plug in \(a = 1\) and find \(T\text{.}\) Does Kepler’s third law give the correct number of days in a year?

    (b)

    Mars’s distance from the sun is 1.524 AU. Use Kepler’s third law to find the length of a year on Mars.

    (c)

    Rewrite Kepler’s Third Law so that it has the form
    \begin{equation*} T = \text{something}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    (d)

    Kepler’s Third Law is a power model in disguise! Talk to your table and decide how to use fractional exponents to rewrite your equation from the previous part with a fractional exponent.

    (e)

    Use Kepler’s Third Law to predict the distance from the Sun to a planet with a 10,776 day year. (What planet is this? Use the Internet to find out! How good was your distance estimate?)

    Activity 5.2.5.

    Pink noise is a type of sound that may help some people fall asleep and/or help people with ADHD concentrate.
     3 
    C. Johnson, “Have you tried pink noise for sleep? Here’s what to know.” AP New, May 20, 2024. https://apnews.com/article/pink-brown-white-noise-sleep-focus-concentration-f5f24dad1effb09c1cf8b607bd22ebc7
    Higher frequency sounds are less intense than low frequency sounds in pink noise. We can model this relationship using the formula
    \begin{equation*} P = \frac{1}{f} \end{equation*}
    where \(P\) is the power of the sound and \(f\) is its frequency.

    (a)

    The two octaves around middle A on a piano have frequencies of about 220 - 880 Hz. Sketch a graph showing the relationship of power to frequency in pink noise for the two octaves around middle A.

    (b)

    The model for pink noise is also a power model! We can rewrite it as
    \begin{equation*} P = f^{-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Talk to your table and brainstorm reasons why it might make sense for negative exponents to be related to variables in the denominator of fractions.

    (c)

    Brown noise is similar to pink noise, but has the model
    \begin{equation*} P = \frac{1}{f^2} \end{equation*}
    instead. How can we rewrite this formula as a power model? Think on your own, and then compare notes with your table.

    (d)

    Sketch a graph showing the relationship of power to frequency in brown noise for the two octaves around middle A.

    (e)

    Does pink or brown noise have more power at 220 Hz? at 440 Hz? at 880 Hz?
